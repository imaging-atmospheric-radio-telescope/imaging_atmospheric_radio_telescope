Simulating an imaging radio-telescope to detect cosmic gamma-rays in earth's atmosphere during the day

Cherenkov-telescopes efficiently detect the rare cosmic gamma-rays with energies above one Terra electron Volt due to their large collecting areas.
However, the atmospheric Cherenkov-method needs dark and clear skies what limits its duty-cycle to about 12 percent (1000 hours per year).
To observe all the time, we explore imaging radio telescopes which operate similar to Cherenkov-telescopes.
Our imaging radio telescopes face the shower anti parallel, have mirrors ranging from 10 to 25 meters in diameter, have an angular resolution of about 0.2 degrees, and create images with about 1k pixels.
We take inspiration from UCHICAGO's MIDAS, and KIT's CROME telescopes, and started to simulate imaging optics based on wave mechanics.
We use CORSIKA-CoREAS to simulate showers and feed their radio-emission into our simulation of an imaging radio-telescope.
In this contribution, we introduce the basics of our telescope and its simulation.
We address its limits and sources of noise, as far as we currently understand them.
We show images of showers taken with our simulated telescopes.
We are looking forward to discuss with the experts in radio-emission in order to test our simulations before we continue to estimate our telescope's energetic threshold and effective collecting areas.
